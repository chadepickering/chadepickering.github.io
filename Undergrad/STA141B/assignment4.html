# query on USDA db, output json object (list of items)
def ndb_search(term):
    # input: term (type chr): the term to search
    # output: json_obj (type list): list with each query item
    
    # url string + inputted search term
    search = "https://api.nal.usda.gov/ndb/search/?format=json&q="+term
    
    # append remainder of url (key info)
    search = search+"&sort=r&offset=0&api_key="+key+"&ds=Standard Reference" # sort = {n, r}
    
    # use caching mechanism and retrieve the data
    req = requests.get(search)
    
    # make json object
    json_obj = req.json()
    
    # return elements of json_obj (as "list", "item") when they are present; o/w empty list when KeyError
    try:
        list_out = json_obj["list"]["item"]
    except KeyError:
        list_out=[]
    return(list_out)

Exercise 1.4. Which foods provide the best combination of price, yield, and nutrition? You can use kilocalories as a measure of "nutrition" here, but more a detailed analysis is better. Use plots to support your analysis.

To assess "nutrition", I will assume rough normality of all numeric variables (checked scatterplot matrix below) in order to standardize and create an additive model. The standardization for price, kcal, and sugar have been adjusted to be the negative versions of what they actually are to make an appropriate scale for each variable (a lower price, lower kcal, and lower sugar amount per serving would be considered more optimal/more nutritious for the food measures, thus a negation of all z-scores is appropriate).

Procedure/Analysis: Here, I check the scatter matrix to first check the distributions of all of the numerical data, but also to identify any potential relationships between variables. The primary purpose of the question is to yield a measure of how good the price and yield of a food is combined with the selected nutrients, but it is worth pointing out a few two-way associations I see from the scatter matrix because if two (or more) variables are correlated with each other, it may introduce a certain exaggeration or bias into the model.

For example, price is at least mildly positively associated with most of the other variables (as price increases, kcal, protein, fiber, and vitamin C tend to as well) - this indicates that when the standard score of price is above 0 (above the mean), the standard scores for those other variables are likely to be above 0 as well. Protein has the same kind of mild positive relationship with kcal and protein that could introduce some minimal bias in the overall standard scores. These biases may produce artificially high (or low) standard scores that could impact a decision to buy certain products based on price, yield, and nutritional value.

Procedure: Finally, after all z-scores are correctly calculated, we sum the scores row-wise to yield a "standard additive score" for each food. This measure is a non-weighted relative index; in other words, for the eight variables we are measuring, these additive scores tell us which foods most satisfy our conditions. The larger scores have the best combinations of the eight variables and the lower scores (negative) have the worst combinations of the eight variables. In other words, the foods that scored the best either had mild to moderately high z-scores in most or all categories that summed to yield high additive scores, or they had extremely high values relative to the mean in only a small subset of the variables but the average to below-average performance in the other variables did not decrease the food's overall additive measure enough to bring it back down.

Note that each of the eight variables are weighted equally here. In a future model for a potential client, for example, weights could be assigned to each of the variables according to their relative importance in the model. Additive weights would shift the mean of the standard scores away from 0 without changing the variance, whereas multiplicative weights would increase or decrease the variance. The method of weighting for each of the eight variables would have to be determined based on the situation.

It should also be noted that all of the z-scores that are added to create our additive standard scores are calculated relative to the body of data that we have now. If more foods were scraped and added to the data frame, we would get a more accurate and complete understanding of the variables and how they interact, as well as how different foods stack up in comparison with one another. We would also be able to identify more important variables from the basic food reports and include those in our model. And, of course, adding weights relative to importance would be a critical and exciting feature of a future model.
